I’ll focus on the **practical surgical flow**, but this should be read as a **supervised training framework**, not a standalone operative manual. Eyelid surgery is high-risk because wrong dissection, excessive fat removal, bleeding, or injury to levator/inferior oblique/orbital structures can cause eyelid deformity or even vision-threatening complications.

## Core principle

The surgery is not simply “remove eyelid fat.” In blepharoplasty, the surgeon usually treats **herniated orbital fat pads**, excess skin, orbicularis laxity, septal weakness, or tear-trough deformity. The upper eyelid has mainly **nasal/medial and central/pre-aponeurotic fat pads**, with the lacrimal gland laterally; the lower eyelid has **medial, central, and lateral fat pads**, and the inferior oblique muscle lies dangerously close between the medial and central lower fat pads. ([EyeWiki][1])

# Practical workflow: upper eyelid fat-removal blepharoplasty

## 1. Preoperative analysis

Before touching the eyelid, the surgeon decides whether the problem is actually fat, skin, brow descent, ptosis, or asymmetry. A common junior mistake is assuming all upper eyelid fullness is fat, when it may be brow ptosis, dermatochalasis, lacrimal gland prolapse, or ethnic eyelid anatomy. EyeWiki emphasizes that upper eyelid crease position, septal attachment, fat descent, and ethnic anatomy affect the surgical plan. ([EyeWiki][1])

Practical checklist:

| Assessment                | Why it matters                                         |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Brow position             | Low brow can mimic upper lid excess                    |
| Levator function / ptosis | Ptosis may need ptosis repair, not only blepharoplasty |
| Skin excess               | Determines skin excision plan                          |
| Medial fat bulge          | Often the main fat-removal target                      |
| Lacrimal gland position   | Must not be mistaken for fat                           |
| Dry eye history           | Surgery can worsen ocular surface symptoms             |
| Eyelid closure            | Poor closure increases exposure risk                   |

## 2. Marking

The patient is marked **awake and upright**, because eyelid fold, brow position, and asymmetry change when the patient lies down. The surgeon marks the natural eyelid crease, then estimates the safe amount of skin to remove while preserving eyelid closure.

Junior concept: **marking is part of the operation, not decoration.** Bad marking can create asymmetry, lagophthalmos, high crease, hollowing, or an unnatural “operated” look.

## 3. Anesthesia and preparation

Upper eyelid blepharoplasty is commonly performed with local anesthesia, sometimes with sedation depending on patient comfort and surgeon preference. Mayo Clinic describes blepharoplasty as a procedure that may remove excess skin, muscle, and fat, and lists risks including infection, bleeding, dry eyes, difficulty closing the eyes, scarring, eye muscle injury, blurred vision, and rare vision loss. ([Mayo Clinic][2])

Practical focus:

| Step                          | Purpose                        |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Local anesthetic infiltration | Analgesia and vasoconstriction |
| Eye protection                | Prevent corneal injury         |
| Sterile prep                  | Reduce infection risk          |
| Symmetry confirmation         | Re-check before incision       |

## 4. Skin incision and skin excision

The surgeon incises along the marked upper eyelid crease and removes the planned skin segment. Depending on the case, a small amount of orbicularis muscle may be preserved, trimmed, or opened, but aggressive muscle removal is avoided because it can worsen hollowing or eyelid function.

Junior concept: **upper blepharoplasty is mostly a contour-balancing operation.** Removing too much skin or muscle can create functional problems; removing too much fat can age the eye.

## 5. Orbital septum exposure

After skin and superficial tissue management, the surgeon identifies the orbital septum. The septum lies deep to skin and orbicularis and anterior to the orbital fat. In the upper lid, the septum inserts on the levator aponeurosis, so careless deep dissection can affect eyelid elevation. ([EyeWiki][1])

Practical rule for juniors: **do not chase fat blindly.** The surgeon exposes only the area needed, usually where the fat pad is visibly prominent.

## 6. Fat pad identification

The surgeon identifies the correct fat compartment. In the upper eyelid, the main surgically relevant fat pads are the **nasal/medial** and **central/pre-aponeurotic** fat pads; the lacrimal gland is lateral and must be protected. ([EyeWiki][1])

Practical differentiation:

| Structure                   | Practical significance                             |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Medial/nasal fat pad        | Common source of upper medial puffiness            |
| Central/pre-aponeurotic fat | Can contribute to central fullness                 |
| Lacrimal gland              | Lateral structure; avoid mistaking it for fat      |
| Levator aponeurosis         | Deep functional structure; injury can cause ptosis |

## 7. Conservative fat reduction

The surgeon gently delivers only the protruding fat and removes or contours it conservatively. The key is to reduce the bulge without hollowing the upper eyelid. A major complication review specifically warns that excess fat removal or an unnaturally high crease can produce a hollowed-out upper eyelid appearance. ([PMC][3])

Practical decision-making:

| Intraoperative finding                    | Better decision                                    |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Mild medial puffiness                     | Small conservative medial fat contouring           |
| Central fullness but good youthful volume | Preserve most central fat                          |
| Hollow superior sulcus already present    | Avoid fat removal; consider volume-preserving plan |
| Lateral fullness                          | Confirm it is not lacrimal gland prolapse          |

## 8. Hemostasis

Bleeding control is critical because the eyelid and orbit are small spaces. ASPS lists bleeding, infection, dry eye, difficulty closing the eyes, ectropion, lid lag, pain, swelling, bruising, and possible revision surgery among eyelid surgery risks. ([American Society of Plastic Surgeons][4])

Practical junior principle: **before closure, the field must be dry and symmetric.** A small postoperative orbital bleed can become a serious emergency.

## 9. Closure

The incision is closed along the natural crease. The surgeon checks eyelid height, crease symmetry, skin tension, and ability to close the eye.

Practical endpoint:

| Good endpoint          | Bad endpoint             |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Smooth crease          | High, unnatural crease   |
| Gentle contour         | Hollow superior sulcus   |
| Full eyelid closure    | Tension or lagophthalmos |
| Symmetric skin removal | Asymmetric fold height   |

# Practical workflow: lower eyelid fat surgery

Lower eyelid surgery is more technically dangerous than upper blepharoplasty because the lower lid can retract, roll outward, or become hollow if fat/skin/support are mishandled.

## 1. Decide: fat removal or fat repositioning?

Modern lower blepharoplasty often emphasizes **less aggressive fat removal**, **fat repositioning**, minimal skin removal, and lower eyelid support when needed. A 2025 lower blepharoplasty safety review states that contemporary approaches emphasize conservative fat removal, fat repositioning, canthopexy/canthoplasty support, and volume augmentation where appropriate. ([PMC][5])

Practical decision:

| Patient type                        | Likely approach                            |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Young patient, fat bags, tight skin | Transconjunctival fat contouring           |
| Fat bags + tear trough hollow       | Fat repositioning rather than pure removal |
| Older patient, skin excess + laxity | Skin management plus possible lid support  |
| Lower lid laxity                    | Consider canthopexy/canthoplasty support   |
| Dry eye / prominent eyes            | More conservative plan                     |

## 2. Lower eyelid preoperative tests

The surgeon checks lower eyelid tone, snap-back, distraction, scleral show, globe prominence, cheek support, and tear-trough depth. EyeWiki notes that safe lower eyelid surgery depends on understanding lower lid anatomy and physiology; the lower eyelid has anterior, middle, and posterior lamellae. ([EyeWiki][6])

Practical meaning:

| Test/finding    | Surgical importance                 |
| --------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Lid laxity      | Higher risk of ectropion/retraction |
| Scleral show    | Avoid aggressive skin removal       |
| Tear trough     | May need fat repositioning          |
| Prominent globe | Higher exposure/retraction risk     |
| Dry eye         | Avoid overcorrection                |

## 3. Choose approach

### Option A: Transconjunctival approach

This is commonly used when the main issue is fat bulging with minimal skin excess. The transconjunctival approach was developed to reduce visible external scarring while allowing access to lower orbital fat pads, and it is described as a good option for pseudoherniated infraorbital fat with little lid laxity. ([PMC][7])

### Option B: Transcutaneous / subciliary approach

This is used when skin and muscle excess also need treatment, but it carries more risk of lower lid malposition if skin is over-resected or lid support is poor. ASPS specifically lists ectropion and lid lag as risks after eyelid surgery. ([American Society of Plastic Surgeons][4])

# Lower transconjunctival fat surgery: practical sequence

## 1. Access from inside the eyelid

The surgeon approaches through the conjunctival side of the lower lid, avoiding an external skin incision. This preserves the anterior lamella and avoids a visible skin scar. ([PMC][7])

Junior concept: this approach is usually selected when the problem is **fat prominence**, not major skin excess.

## 2. Enter the correct surgical plane

The goal is to reach the orbital fat pads safely without damaging the lower lid retractors, conjunctiva, or extraocular muscles. The lower eyelid has medial, central, and lateral fat pads, and the inferior oblique muscle lies between the medial and central fat pads, making that zone especially important. ([PMC][8])

Practical warning:

| Danger zone                         | Why it matters                          |
| ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Between medial and central fat pads | Inferior oblique muscle risk            |
| Excessive posterior dissection      | Diplopia/extraocular muscle injury risk |
| Aggressive fat traction             | Bleeding and contour deformity risk     |
| Excess fat removal                  | Hollow lower eyelid risk                |

## 3. Identify the fat compartments

The surgeon determines which fat pad is creating the visible bag. Lower eyelid contour depends on three retroseptal fat pads; not every pad needs treatment. ([PMC][8])

Practical example:

| Visible deformity      | Likely target                   |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Medial under-eye bulge | Medial fat pad                  |
| Central under-eye bag  | Central fat pad                 |
| Lateral fullness       | Lateral fat pad                 |
| Bag + groove below     | Fat repositioning may be better |

## 4. Conservative fat contouring

If the patient has true fat excess without hollowing, the surgeon conservatively reduces the protruding fat. The goal is a smooth transition, not a flat or hollow lower eyelid.

Junior principle: **the lower lid is unforgiving.** Small over-resection can create long-term hollowing.

## 5. Fat repositioning when tear trough is present

If there is a tear-trough groove, the surgeon may preserve and reposition fat over the orbital rim instead of discarding it. Literature on lower eyelid blepharoplasty describes fat relocation as a method to correct inferior orbital hollowing. ([PMC][8])

Practical example:
A patient with a bulging lower lid and deep groove below the bag should not automatically receive fat removal. The bag may need to be blended into the groove using fat repositioning, otherwise the groove can look deeper after surgery.

## 6. Skin management if needed

If skin excess remains, the surgeon may use conservative skin pinch, resurfacing, or a transcutaneous method depending on anatomy. Modern lower blepharoplasty favors minimal skin removal and support when needed because aggressive skin removal can pull the lid downward. ([PMC][5])

## 7. Lower lid support decision

If preoperative laxity is present, the surgeon may add lid support such as canthopexy or canthoplasty. This is not cosmetic decoration; it is structural risk control to reduce ectropion or retraction.

# Practical example: young patient with lower eyelid bags

**Patient:** 32-year-old man with lower eyelid bags, good skin tone, no lid laxity, no major tear trough.

**Practical plan:** transconjunctival lower blepharoplasty with conservative fat contouring.

**Surgical logic:**

| Step                                                 | Practical reason                            |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Confirm no lid laxity                                | Reduces malposition risk                    |
| Use internal approach                                | No external scar; good for fat-only problem |
| Identify medial/central/lateral pads                 | Treat only the bulging compartments         |
| Protect inferior oblique region                      | Avoid diplopia risk                         |
| Remove minimal protruding fat                        | Avoid hollowing                             |
| No skin excision or very conservative skin treatment | Skin elasticity is good                     |

**Teaching point:** This is a “fat bag” case, not a skin-excess case.

# Practical example: older patient with bags and hollow tear trough

**Patient:** 56-year-old woman with lower eyelid bags, tear-trough hollowing, mild skin excess, and mild lower lid laxity.

**Practical plan:** lower blepharoplasty with fat repositioning, conservative skin management, and possible lower lid support.

**Surgical logic:**

| Step                         | Practical reason                 |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Evaluate lid laxity          | Decide whether support is needed |
| Avoid pure fat removal       | Would worsen hollowing           |
| Reposition fat               | Smooth bag-to-cheek transition   |
| Conservative skin management | Avoid lid pull-down              |
| Add support if laxity exists | Reduce ectropion/retraction risk |

**Teaching point:** In this patient, the best result usually comes from **redistribution and support**, not simple fat removal.

# Most important “do not do” rules for juniors

| Mistake                          | Result                                  |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Removing too much upper fat      | Hollow, aged upper eyelid               |
| Mistaking lacrimal gland for fat | Dry eye, lateral contour deformity      |
| Ignoring brow ptosis             | Poor result despite good eyelid surgery |
| Removing too much lower fat      | Hollow under-eye deformity              |
| Removing too much lower skin     | Lid retraction or ectropion             |
| Ignoring lower lid laxity        | Postoperative malposition               |
| Poor hemostasis                  | Hematoma, vision-threatening emergency  |
| Treating all patients the same   | Asymmetry and unnatural results         |

## Final practical summary

For upper eyelids, the practical sequence is: **diagnose skin/fat/brow/ptosis → mark awake → conservative skin excision → expose septum only where needed → identify medial/central fat safely → remove minimal protruding fat → meticulous hemostasis → crease closure and symmetry check**.

For lower eyelids, the practical sequence is: **assess lid support and tear trough → choose transconjunctival or transcutaneous access → identify medial/central/lateral fat pads → protect inferior oblique region → decide fat removal versus repositioning → manage skin conservatively → add lid support if needed → monitor closely for bleeding, dry eye, retraction, ectropion, or vision symptoms**.
